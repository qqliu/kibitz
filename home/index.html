About

Kibitz offers two different ways to build your recommenders and two different types of recommenders. To start, follow the following steps:

  1. Upload your table of items on Datahub. Click the Sign-Up link and allow Kibitz access to your table.

  2. Fill in information about your table: we will display a title column, description column, and an image column by default.

  3. If you have a column that contains a numerical ranking of your items, you may include it. We will provide recommendations of items with the highest rankings (i.e. an item with a ranking of 10 will be recommended before an item of ranking 2). Duplicate rankings are allowed!

  4. Submit your information, and we create a default recommender for you! To view more customization options, log in to Kibitz using your Datahub account.

Creation Options

Ready-Built

Upload a table to Datahub with a primary key. Link your table with Kibitz, and Kibitz will give you the option of either downloading a ZIP file containing your recommender to host at your own domain or hosting your recommender at kibitz.csail.mit.edu/YOUR_USERNAME/YOUR_REPO_NAME.

Customize

Customize your recommender using custom table columns. Your table must still contain a primary key. If you choose to use custom table columns, you will need to code your own recommender using our, easy to learn, Kibitz API.

Recommender Types

Mix, match, or use all three! The default recommender will recommend items based on user ratings and overall ranks.

User Ratings

Users create profiles and rate items. We will recommend items based on these user ratings.

Similar Items

We will provide recommendations of items that are similar to items that users like. You must provide us with a customized user similarity file. See our API for more details. (Coming soon!)

Overall Ranks

Given a column of numerical ranks of the items, the recommender will return the items with the highest rankings.

About

Kibitz began as a research project in Haystack, a lab in MIT CSAIL. The purpose of the project is three-fold:
  1. Study the end-to-end application building process of non/inexperienced coders.
  2. Look into whether recommendations may be made for seemingly unrelated items.
  3. Provide an end-to-end backend recommendation builder and engine with easy-to-use API that may be plugged into a variety of frontends.
